Explicit commitments: 
- Euchromatin is the active part of the chromosome that is loose and uncoiled, allowing for gene expression and transcription.
- Barr bodies, on the other hand, are inactive X chromosomes found in female mammals, which are tightly coiled and condensed.
- Heterochromatin is the inactive part of the chromosome that is tightly coiled and condensed
- Barr bodies are actually condensed X chromosomes that become inactivated in female mammals to compensate for the extra X chromosome they possess
- cistron refers to a section of DNA that codes for a single polypeptide
- Cistrons or genes are typically found in active chromatin
-pseudogenes are inactive genes that do not produce functional proteins.
- The Barr body itself is a physical structure of a non-functioning X chromosome, not a pseudogene
Implicit commitments:
- Euchromatin and heterochromatin are mutually exclusive states of chromosome structure, condensation, and activation
- A Barr body is categorically distinct from individual DNA sequences or single genes such as cistrons or pseudogenes
Logical principles: 
- If a biological structure X (in this case a Barr body) exhibits the physical and functional characteristics of category Y (in this case heterochromatin, meaning being tightly coiled, condensed, and inactive), then X is an example of Y.
- If structure X's characteristics contradict the defining features of category Z, X cannot be classified as Z.
